The session

Tutor turns are mint-edged; the student is coral. The student never saw the tutor's instructions, so it can't play along — it stalls, hedges, and self-deprecates like a real anxious beginner.

1Tutor
Welcome. I'm going to tutor you through the first track in this repo — "Anatomy of Claude Code." It's a structural tour: we take the machine apart one piece at a time so the words (context, CLAUDE.md, tools, hooks) start to actually mean something.
Here's how this works, and it's the important part: I won't tick a module off just because I explained it well. I'll tick it when *you* can explain it back to me and answer a quick check. That's the whole deal — understanding I can verify, not understanding I assumed.
Here's our checklist for the track:
1. The Model and the Harness — the LLM underneath vs. the thing wrapped around it
2. The System Prompt — the hidden instructions that make it act like an agent
3. Context — everything the model can see this turn; finite, fills up
4. CLAUDE.md — the context file *you* write
5. Tools (and MCP) — what lets it *do* things, not just talk
6. Sessions, Turns, Hooks — the dialogue, and what you wire into the gaps
We'll go in order, one at a time. Six modules, but they build fast once the bottom layer clicks.
One quick thing before we start, and there's genuinely no wrong answer here. When I check that something landed, do you want me to:
- Ask you to explain it in your own words — I pose a question, you take a swing at it cold, and
- Give you a few options to react to — I lay out some choices, you pick one, and then we talk through *why* it's the right one
Some people think better against a blank page; some think better against a set of options. Either way I'll make sure it actually sticks, and you can switch lanes anytime mid-session. Which sounds more comfortable to start?
2Student
Um, okay. Can I be honest? I think I'd rather have the options to react to. The blank page thing kind of stresses me out — I get worried I'll say something totally wrong and look stupid. So having a few choices to pick from feels safer to me, at least starting out.
Sorry if that's the harder one for you. We can go with that?
3Tutor
No need to apologize at all — you picked the lane that's going to make you learn more, not less, because you won't be sitting there frozen. We'll start with options to react to, and the deal is just: after you pick, I'll ask you to say *why* it's right in your own words. That's the part that makes it stick. And the moment you want to swing at a blank question instead, just say so.
So, options-lane it is. Let's start at the bottom of the machine.
Module 1: The Model and the Harness.
Before I explain anything — just from the title, what do you think "the model" and "the harness" might be? Take a guess, even a loose one. There's no wrong answer here; I just want to see where you're starting from so I'm not explaining stuff you already know.
4Student
Oh, okay, um. So just guessing from the words — "the model" I think is like... the actual AI brain part? Like the thing that's actually smart and knows stuff, like GPT or Claude itself. The intelligence part.
And "the harness"... honestly I'm less sure. A harness is like the straps you put on a dog or a horse, right? So maybe it's the thing that like, holds the model in place and controls it? Like the stuff around it that tells it what to do and keeps it from running off? I really don't know though, I might be totally off on that one. Is that close at all?
5Tutor
Good — and honestly, your guess on the harness is closer than you think. Let me sharpen both.
You're right that the model is the AI brain — a Claude model (Opus, Sonnet, Haiku). But here's the part that surprises people: on its own, the model does *exactly one thing* — it reads some text and predicts what text comes next. That's it. It can't open a file, run a command, or remember yesterday. It's a very good guesser about language, full stop.
So your instinct about the harness — "the stuff around it that controls it and keeps it from running off" — is the right shape. Let me make it concrete. The harness is everything that isn't the model. When the model says "I'd like to read data/trials.tsv," the model can't actually do that — it just produced the *words*. The harness is what goes and actually opens the file, then hands the contents back to the model as more text. The harness assembles what the model sees, runs the tools, manages the back-and-forth. Claude Code *is* the harness.
The metaphor the module uses: the model is the engine, the harness is the car — the wheels, steering, dashboard. You don't drive a bare engine. You drive a car.
Here's the line that makes this the foundation of the whole tour: when people say "the AI did X" or "the AI forgot Y," the interesting part is usually *not the model* — it's the harness. The model didn't remember your project; the harness fed it a file. The model didn't decide to run your tests; it emitted a request, and the harness ran it.
Let me check it landed. Pick the one that's right:
A. The model can run commands and read files by itself; the harness just makes it faster
B. The model only turns text into more text; the harness is what actually reads files, runs commands, and feeds results back to the model
C. The harness is the smart part that does the reasoning; the model just stores data
D. The model and the harness are two names for the same thing — Claude Code
Pick a letter, then tell me *why* you picked it in your own words.
6Student
B.
I picked B because that's basically what you just explained — the model can only do the text thing, like predict what words come next, but it can't actually go open a file or run anything on its own. It just... says it wants to. And then the harness is the part that actually goes and does it and brings the answer back.
A felt wrong because you said the model can't run commands by itself at all, so the harness isn't just making it "faster," it's the thing actually doing the doing. And C kind of flips it backwards — it makes the harness sound like the smart one, but the model's the brain, the harness is more like the hands? And D just seemed off because you made a point of saying they're different things, the engine versus the car.
Is that right? Did I get the why part okay too?
7Tutor
Yes — both the pick and the why. You nailed the part most people skip: you didn't just say "B is right," you said *why each wrong one is wrong*, and your reasons were the real reasons. "The harness isn't making it faster, it's the thing doing the doing" — exactly. And "model's the brain, harness is the hands" is a genuinely good compression of C's mistake. D you caught on the engine-vs-car distinction. That's mastery, not luck.
Checklist now:
✓1. The Model and the Harness — explained back + checked
2. The System Prompt
3. Context
4. CLAUDE.md
5. Tools (and MCP)
6. Sessions, Turns, Hooks
One last thing before we move up a layer, and it's the part that makes this stick beyond a quiz: point this at something real. Last time you used Claude Code, or any AI coding assistant — was there a moment where it "knew" something about your project, or "forgot" something, or "ran" something for you? Pick one and tell me: was that the model, or the harness? Don't overthink it — even a half-memory works.
8Student
Oh, um, okay. Yeah, actually — last week I was using Claude Code on this little Python script, and at one point I asked it about a function and it like, knew the function I meant even though I didn't paste it. So that was... the harness? Because it must've gone and read the file for me, since the model can't do that on its own?
And then later it like, totally forgot a thing I'd told it earlier in the same chat, which felt weird. I'm less sure on that one — is forgetting the harness too? Like the harness didn't feed it back the old part? Or is that the model? I keep wanting to blame the model but you said it's usually the harness, so... harness?
9Tutor
Good instinct, and you got both right — let me make sure the second one rests on solid ground, because "forgetting" is where the context idea (Module 3) is hiding.
The "knew the function I didn't paste" one: yes, harness. The model can't reach into your file on its own — it asked, and the harness opened the file and fed the contents back as text. Spot on.
The forgetting one — you reasoned your way to the right answer, but let me sharpen *why*, because "blame the harness because the tutor said so" isn't quite the grip I want you to have. The model genuinely has no memory between turns. None. Every turn, all it sees is the block of text the harness hands it. So when it "forgot" the thing you said earlier, what actually happened is that thing wasn't in the block of text it got that turn — the harness either dropped it or summarized it away to make room. So yes, it's the harness, but the deeper reason is: *the model never remembers anything on its own — remembering is entirely the harness's job of putting the right text back in front of it.* That's a near-perfect setup for the next module, which is exactly about that block of text.
You held a real example against the model/harness line and it bent the right way. That's the transfer I wanted.
Now — given the session's pacing, here's where I'll be straight with you. We'd keep going exactly like this: Module 2 (the System Prompt — the hidden instructions, written by Anthropic not you, that make the model *act* like an agent), then Module 3 (Context — that "block of text" we just kept bumping into, finite and filling up), and on through CLAUDE.md, Tools, and Hooks. Same loop every time: your guess first, then options to react to, then you tell me the why, then we point it at your real work. The lane stays yours, and you can switch to blank-page questions whenever you feel like it.
Since you've genuinely earned Module 1 — both the explanation and a real example — let me close with the one thing that ties this whole track together, and it's a question you can already answer:
In one sentence — when something seems "magic" about Claude Code (it knew, it forgot, it ran something), what's the single question you should ask to make it stop being magic?
